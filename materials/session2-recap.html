Square names
The coordinate system

Every square has a unique name: a file letter (a–h) and a rank number (1–8). Files run left to right from White's perspective. Ranks run bottom to top. Always write the file first, then the rank — so the bottom-left corner is a1, and the top-right is h8.

Memory trick: "Light on right" — when setting up, the bottom-right corner (h1) must always be a light square. If it is dark, the board is rotated incorrectly.
Pieces on the board
  • Files (columns) — a through h, left to right
  • Ranks (rows) — 1 through 8, White's side to Black's side
  • a1 is always the bottom-left dark square
  • h8 is always the top-right dark square
  • White pieces start on ranks 1 and 2; Black on ranks 7 and 8
  • The centre squares are e4, e5, d4, d5 — the most important area of the board

2
Points system
  • Pawn = 1 pt — the soldier; individually weak but powerful in groups
  • Knight = 3 pts — unique L-shape, only piece that jumps
  • Bishop = 3 pts — long diagonal, but stuck on one colour forever
  • Rook = 5 pts — most powerful open-line piece, vital in endgames
  • Queen = 9 pts — combines rook and bishop; most powerful piece
  • King = priceless — no point value; losing it ends the game

Good trades & bad trades
The decision framework

Before every capture, ask: "What do I give up, and what do I get?" Subtract the value of what you lose from the value of what you gain. A positive result is good; negative is bad.

Kingside vs Queenside
How the board is divided

The board is split into two halves. The Kingside is the half where both kings start (files e, f, g, h). The Queenside is the half where both queens start (files a, b, c, d). This division is permanent — it does not change even after pieces move.

♛ Queenside — files a, b, c, d
The queen's half. O-O-O = queenside castle. The king travels further (3 squares). The rook ends up on d1 — very active.
♚ Kingside — files e, f, g, h
The king's half. O-O = kingside castle. The king travels 2 squares to g1. Most common castle — fewer pieces need to move first.

Castling — all 7 rules with diagrams
What castling is

Castling is the only move in chess where two pieces move at once. The king moves two squares towards a rook, and that rook jumps to the square the king crossed. It is one single move — not two. Kingside castle: King e1→g1, Rook h1→f1. Queenside castle: King e1→c1, Rook a1→d1.
