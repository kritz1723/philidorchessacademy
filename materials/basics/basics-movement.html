How Pieces Move

In every diagram below, the piece sits on d4 and the green squares show where it can go on an empty board. A piece captures by moving onto a square occupied by an enemy — replacing it. Pieces cannot move through their own men, and only the Knight may jump over other pieces.

♖ Rook — straight lines

The Rook moves any number of squares horizontally or vertically — along ranks and files. It is at its best on open lines with no pawns in the way.

♗ Bishop — diagonals

The Bishop moves any number of squares diagonally. Because it never changes colour, each side has one light-squared and one dark-squared Bishop.

♕ Queen — lines and diagonals

The Queen combines Rook and Bishop — she moves any distance straight or diagonally. This makes her the most powerful piece on the board.

♘ Knight — the L-shape

The Knight moves in an L: two squares in one direction, then one square at a right angle. It is the only piece that jumps over others, and it always lands on the opposite colour to the one it left.

♔ King — one step

The King moves one square in any direction. He is slow but must always be kept safe — he can never move onto a square attacked by an enemy piece.

♙ Pawn — forward, captures sideways

A Pawn moves straight forward one square — or two squares on its very first move. But it captures diagonally, one square ahead. Here the e4-pawn may advance to e5, or capture either black pawn on d5 or f5.

Promotion: a Pawn that reaches the far end of the board is promoted — exchanged for a Queen, Rook, Bishop or Knight of the same colour. Almost always a Queen.
