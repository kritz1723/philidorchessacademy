Check & Checkmate
Check — the King is attacked

When a piece attacks the enemy King, the King is in check. Check is not the end — but you must respond immediately and get out of it. You may never leave your own King in check, and you can never move into check.

There are exactly three ways to escape a check:

  • Move the King to a safe square.
  • Block — put a piece between the King and the attacker (works against Rooks, Bishops, Queens, not Knights).
  • Capture the checking piece.
Checkmate — check with no escape

If a King is in check and there is no legal way to escape — can't move, block, or capture — it is checkmate and the game ends at once.

Here the black King on g8 is checked by the rook on e8 along the back rank. Its only escape squares (f8, h8) are still on the rank, and its own pawns on f7/g7/h7 block the way forward. No escape — checkmate.

This pattern — a King trapped by its own pawns on the back rank — is called back-rank mate. It is one of the most common ways beginners win (and lose!).
Stalemate — a draw, not a win

If the player to move is not in check but has no legal move at all, the game is a stalemate — an immediate draw.

Here it is Black to move. The King on h8 is not in check, but every square around it (g7, g8, h7) is covered by the white Queen. With no legal move, the game is drawn — even though White is hugely ahead.

Watch out: when you are winning easily, give the losing King a safe square. Accidentally stalemating throws away the win.
